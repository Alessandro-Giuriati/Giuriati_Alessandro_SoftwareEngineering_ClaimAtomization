Extracted claims:

1. The BMW M3 helped bring high-performance rear-wheel-drive cars into the mainstream.
2. The E90-generation BMW M3 was sold from 2007 to 2013.
3. The E90-generation M3 used a V8 engine.
4. The E90-generation M3 was offered with three body styles and two gearboxes.
5. The E90-generation M3 can now be bought for less than £20,000.
6. The fourth-generation M3’s move from six to eight cylinders pushed its price above £50,000.
7. The E90-generation M3 had a 414bhp 4.0-litre naturally aspirated V8 engine.
8. It was available with a six-speed manual gearbox or an optional seven-speed DCT twin-clutch transmission.
9. The E90-generation M3 could do 0-60mph in under five seconds, 0-100mph in ten seconds, and had a 155mph limited top speed.
10. The E92 coupe launched first, followed by the E93 folding hard-top cabrio and the E90 four-door saloon.
11. Coupes accounted for two-thirds of the 8,262 M3s sold in the UK.
12. The coupe weighed 1,655kg.
13. The convertible weighed 150kg more than the coupe.
14. The E90 saloon came to the UK in a total of 625 examples.
15. The E90 M3 used a “power dome” bonnet, M-badged side vents, bulging wheel arches, quad exhaust pipes, and a more intricate front bumper than a standard 3 Series.
16. Inside, the M3 had an M-branded gear knob or DCT selector, bespoke dials with a light-up red line, and a steering wheel button marked “M”.
17. The M button could alter stability control, adjustable suspension firmness, and gearshift paddle response.
18. Without the M button pressed, the M3 behaved much like a standard BMW 3 Series.
19. The E90 M3’s hydraulic steering was more natural in its responses than the current turbocharged M3’s electronic setup.
20. The E90 M3’s engine was naturally aspirated, while its successor and most other performance cars at that level had moved to turbocharging.
21. The M3’s engine revved to 8,300rpm.
22. The DCT gearbox was available with five shift settings.
23. The DCT gearbox outsold the manual gearbox over the M3’s life.
24. The E90 M3 is less terrifying in poor road conditions than the current M3 because it lacks the instant punch of two turbos.
25. The engine produced 414bhp and 295lb ft to the rear axle.
26. The dashboard layout had aged, but the interior controls remained easy to get used to.
27. BMW specialist Munich Legends’ director Dan Norris called the E90 M3 the best all-round M Car BMW has ever made.
28. Norris said he had driven his black M3 hard for 50,000 miles, including on track, and it had never gone wrong.
29. A common fault is failure of the two throttle bodies.
30. If one throttle body fails, both should be replaced together.
31. Brand new throttle bodies are recommended over refurbished ones.
32. Each throttle body costs £650, and replacing both costs around £1,800 including VAT.
33. A second major issue is bottom-end engine failure.
34. The E90 M3’s V8 is essentially a cut-down version of the contemporary M5 V10.
35. Engine problems are more likely when the car has been revved hard from cold or poorly maintained.
36. Engines can last beyond 100,000 miles, but neglect can require major work at 50,000 miles.
37. The M3 uses a condition-based servicing system.
38. The V8 typically needs an annual oil change service at around £250.
39. A more detailed service with filters and spark plugs is needed every 40,000 to 50,000 miles and costs around £700.
40. The car’s running-in fluids should have been changed at 1,000 miles.
41. The DCT gearbox can leak oil.
42. Fixing a DCT oil leak costs at least £500 if the gearbox is not removed.
43. The exhaust downpipes can leak and the manifolds can crack, especially on modified cars or those with aftermarket exhaust work.
44. The M3 typically returns around 18mpg when driven hard and around 30mpg when driven gently.
45. A reasonable real-world expectation is 22-24mpg.
46. The car uses about a litre of oil every 4,000 to 5,000 miles if driven sensibly.
47. The correct oil is Castrol 10W-60 fully synthetic approved for BMW M Division cars.
48. Premium tyres cost about £250 each and can last 15,000 to 20,000 miles if used carefully.
49. The brakes are strong but not especially impressive in absolute terms, and track use would call for upgrades.
50. Early 2007 E92 M3 coupes start at just under £17,000.
51. At about £20,000, buyers can choose from all body styles and gearboxes.
52. At about £25,000, buyers can find Competition Pack cars, post-facelift cars, and most examples have 50,000 miles or less.
53. Cars near £30,000 often have very low mileage or high-spec individual colour schemes.
54. M3 GT3 and M3 CRT models cost £120,000 or more.
55. Richard Hodge’s E90 M3 is his third M3, after an E36 and an E46.
56. Hodge bought his August 2010 E90 M3 in 2013.
57. He chose the four-door version because he preferred its look, found it more discreet, and saw very few of them.
58. He likes the naturally aspirated V8’s performance and sound.
59. He thinks the handling is sublime and slightly better than the E92 coupe’s.
60. He chose the DCT gearbox because it is easy in traffic and works well in manual mode.
61. He says the car is not cheap to run, but servicing costs are reasonable.
62. He struggles to get 20mpg in his car.
63. He has owned the car for over three years and does not want to replace it.
64. He thinks the latest turbocharged M3 is not as good as his car.
