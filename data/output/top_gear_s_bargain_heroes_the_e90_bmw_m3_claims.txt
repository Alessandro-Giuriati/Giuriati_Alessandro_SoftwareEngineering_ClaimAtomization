Extracted claims:

1. The BMW M3 is credited with bringing high-performance rear-wheel-drive cars into the mainstream.
2. The E90-series BMW M3 was sold from 2007 to 2013.
3. The E90-series M3 used a V8 engine and came in three body styles with a choice of two gearboxes.
4. BMW’s fourth-generation M3 marked a jump from six to eight cylinders and pushed the price above £50,000.
5. The E90 M3’s 4.0-litre V8 produced 414bhp.
6. The E90 M3 offered a six-speed manual gearbox or an optional seven-speed DCT twin-clutch transmission.
7. The E90 M3 could do 0-60mph in under five seconds, 0-100mph in ten seconds, and had a 155mph limited top speed.
8. The E92 coupe launched first, followed by the E93 folding hard-top cabriolet and the E90 four-door saloon.
9. Two-thirds of the 8,262 M3s sold in the UK were coupes.
10. The coupe was the lightest body style in this generation at 1,655kg.
11. The convertible carried a 150kg weight penalty.
12. Just 625 E90-series saloons came to the UK.
13. The E90 M3’s external distinguishing features include a bonnet power dome, M-badged side vents, bulging wheel arches, quad exhaust pipes, and a more intricate front bumper than a standard 3 Series.
14. Inside, it has an M-branded gear knob or selector, bespoke dials with a light-up red line, and a steering wheel button marked “M”.
15. That M button lets the driver preselect settings for stability control, optional adjustable suspension, and shift-paddle response.
16. Without its performance settings engaged, the M3 behaves like a normal BMW 3 Series and is refined and easy to drive.
17. The hydraulic steering is more natural than the electronic steering in the current turbocharged M3.
18. The engine is a naturally aspirated V8 that builds to a screaming sound above 8,000rpm.
19. The DCT gearbox had five settings and was outsold by the manual over the M3’s life.
20. The V8 predecessor is less terrifying than the current M3 in wet, grimy, or icy conditions.
21. The E90 M3 sends 414bhp and 295lb ft to the rear axle.
22. The E90 M3’s main mechanical weak point is its two throttle bodies.
23. If one throttle body fails, the car typically goes into limp-home mode.
24. Replacement of both throttle bodies costs around £1,800 including VAT.
25. The V8 can suffer bottom-end failure, especially if it is revved hard from cold or poorly maintained.
26. Engines can last beyond 100,000 miles, but unsympathetic ownership can require major work at 50,000 miles.
27. The V8 needs annual oil changes at around £250 and a more detailed service every 40,000 to 50,000 miles for about £700.
28. The car should have had its running-in fluids changed at 1,000 miles.
29. The DCT gearbox can leak oil, and fixing it costs at least £500.
30. The exhaust downpipes can leak and the manifolds can crack, especially on modified cars or cars with aftermarket exhausts.
31. A hard-driven E90 M3 returns about 18mpg, while gentle driving can approach 30mpg.
32. A typical mixed-use fuel economy is 22-24mpg.
33. The car uses about a litre of oil every 4,000 to 5,000 miles when driven sensibly.
34. Premium tyres cost about £250 each and may last 15,000 to 20,000 miles if treated carefully.
35. Early E92 M3 coupes from 2007 start at just under £17,000.
36. At around £20,000, buyers can choose among the body styles and gearboxes.
37. At around £25,000, cars with the Competition Pack and post-facelift models become available, with most having 50,000 miles or less.
38. Cars near £30,000 tend to have very low mileage or high-spec individual options and special colour schemes.
39. The M3 GT3 and M3 CRT cost £120,000 or more.

Total claims extracted: 39
